## Why NCRs Matter

A Non-Conformance Report (NCR) is your official record that something doesn't meet the project specification, drawing, or applicable standard. A well-written NCR protects the project — and protects **you**.

In my experience, the biggest problem isn't finding non-conformances. It's documenting them clearly enough that the contractor actually understands what needs to be fixed.

---

## The Anatomy of a Good NCR

### 1. Clear Description of the Non-Conformance
Be specific. Don't write:

❌ *"Cable installation not correct."*

Write:

✅ *"3-core 16mm² XLPE cable installed in cable tray at Grid A-3 without proper de-rating — cable tray fill exceeds 40% per project spec MR-EC-001 Rev 2, Clause 5.4.3."*

### 2. Reference the Applicable Document
Always cite:
- Project specification number + revision
- Drawing number
- Applicable standard (IEC, SAES, NFPA, etc.)

### 3. Attach Photographic Evidence
One clear photo is worth ten paragraphs. Always attach site photos with the NCR — date-stamped if possible.

### 4. Define the Required Corrective Action
Don't leave it vague. State exactly what needs to happen:

✅ *"Contractor to re-route cable or provide approved de-rating calculation signed by a qualified engineer. Revised ITP inspection required before backfill."*

### 5. Set a Realistic Closure Date
Give the contractor a fair deadline — but follow up. An open NCR at mechanical completion is a headache nobody wants.

---

## Tips from the Field

- **Number your NCRs sequentially** — makes tracking and reporting easy
- **Never close an NCR without a physical re-inspection** — paper alone is not enough
- **Keep a log** (Excel works fine) — NCR number, date raised, discipline, status, closure date
- **Be professional, not personal** — the NCR is against the work, not the person

---

## Final Thought

A clean NCR register at project handover is one of the best things you can leave behind. It shows the client that quality was actively managed — not just rubber-stamped.
